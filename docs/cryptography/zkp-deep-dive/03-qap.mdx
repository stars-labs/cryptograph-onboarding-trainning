## Why do we need yet another representation?

Last section we learned R1CS:

- Each constraint is like a "checklist item"
- As many key multiplications in the circuit, as many constraints
- If every one passes, the whole circuit is correct

So far so good.

**But what if there are 10, 1,000, or 100,000 constraints?**

The verifier can't check them one by one.

QAP's purpose can be summed up in one line:

> **Compress "many small checks" into "one big check."**

:::info Grade-school analogy
Imagine a teacher has to check your 100 arithmetic problems:
- **R1CS**: Is problem 1 right? Is problem 2 right? Check each one.
- **QAP**: Compress all 100 problems into a single "overall score report." If that one report satisfies a rule, all 100 problems were right.
:::

---

## What is QAP actually doing?

QAP is not about inventing new constraints. Its core moves are:

1. Keep the information in R1CS
2. Turn "each column of numbers" in the matrices into "a polynomial"
3. Turn "many constraints all hold" into "one polynomial divides another polynomial"

If you only remember one sentence, remember this:

> **R1CS checks row by row. QAP packs all rows together and checks once.**

---

## Key question: why turn numbers into curves at all?

This is where students get stuck most often: the table was fine — why bother turning it into polynomials?

One-line answer:

> **Tables of numbers can't be "combined," but polynomials can.**

For example:

- You have two Excel tables. There's **no way** to "multiply" them into a new table.
- But two polynomials can be multiplied directly: $(t+1) \cdot (t-2) = t^2 - t - 2$, and the result is still a polynomial.

QAP wants to combine "all 100 constraints hold" into "one polynomial equation holds." To combine, we first need the numbers in each constraint to become objects that support arithmetic — polynomials.

---

## Step 1: think of each column as a few "check-in points"

Recall R1CS:
- Each **row** is a constraint
- Each **column** is a variable (for example the constant 1, x, y, sym₁, sym₂)

From the previous section, the column for variable $x$ in matrix A is:

$$
A_x = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

What does this say?

- In constraint 1, the coefficient of $x$ is 1
- In constraint 2, the coefficient of $x$ is 0
- In constraint 3, the coefficient of $x$ is 1

Label the three constraints as three evaluation points:

$$
t = 1,\; 2,\; 3
$$

Now this column reads as:

| Position t | Value |
|--------|----|
| 1 | 1 |
| 2 | 0 |
| 3 | 1 |

So we now have 3 points:

$$
(1,1),\; (2,0),\; (3,1)
$$

---

## Quick warm-up: how many points determine what kind of curve?

Before we touch Lagrange, let's build one piece of intuition.

| How many points? | What curve is determined? |
|---|---|
| 1 point | Infinitely many curves pass through it — not unique |
| **2 points** | **Exactly one straight line** (degree-1 polynomial) |
| **3 points** | **Exactly one parabola** (degree-2 polynomial) |
| n points | **Exactly one degree-(n−1) polynomial curve** |

:::info Temperature log analogy
Monday 10°, Wednesday 0°, Friday 10°.

Roughly what was Tuesday and Thursday? — Draw a smooth curve through those three points and read off the values.

**That's exactly what Lagrange interpolation does: given a few discrete samples, it draws the unique smooth curve through them.**
:::

Back to our 3 points $(1,1),\; (2,0),\; (3,1)$: because there are 3 of them, they determine a **unique degree-2 polynomial curve**.

:::tip Go play with the demo
Now is a good time to go back to the **"Points → Curve Intuition"** tab, drag the sliders to change the heights of the three points, and watch how the curve "wobbles but always passes through the three points."
:::

## Step 2: treat Lagrange as a "trendline" button

OK, we know 3 points uniquely determine a degree-2 curve. So **how do we actually compute that curve?**

The answer is a standard tool called **Lagrange interpolation**. Think of it like Excel's "Add Trendline" feature — feed in the points, it spits out a curve that passes through all of them.

:::tip You don't need to compute this by hand
In real zk-SNARK projects, this step is done automatically by the proving system (the computer).

You only need to accept one fact: **given n points, Lagrange produces a unique degree-(n−1) polynomial.**
:::

For our 3 points $(1,1),\; (2,0),\; (3,1)$, Lagrange gives:

$$
A_x(t) = t^2 - 4t + 4
$$

### Not sure? Just plug in numbers to verify

To confirm "this curve really does pass through those 3 points," **you don't need to understand the formula** — just elementary arithmetic:

| Plug in | Compute $t^2 - 4t + 4$ | Result | Target | OK? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $t = 1$ | $1 - 4 + 4$ | $1$ | $1$ | ✅ |
| $t = 2$ | $4 - 8 + 4$ | $0$ | $0$ | ✅ |
| $t = 3$ | $9 - 12 + 4$ | $1$ | $1$ | ✅ |

The curve does pass through all three points. **At this point you've grasped the most important step of QAP.**

### How to think about what just happened

Previously we recorded:

> "Row 1 is 1, row 2 is 0, row 3 is 1."

Now we record instead:

> "There's a curve that takes the values 1, 0, 1 at positions $t=1, 2, 3$ respectively."

In other words:

- **R1CS records the cells in the table**
- **QAP records the curve that passes through those cells**

:::info Why bother?
Because "many cells" can't be compressed, but "many curves" can be combined via polynomial arithmetic.
That's exactly the "Key question" from earlier — polynomials multiply and add, tables don't.
:::

<details>
<summary>[Optional] How does Lagrange actually construct this curve? (only if you care about the math)</summary>

The core idea is actually simple: **first build "indicator polynomials," then weight-sum them by the y-values.**

**Step 1**: Build a polynomial $L_1(t)$ such that it equals $1$ at $t=1$ and equals $0$ at $t=2, 3$.

- To make it zero at $t=2, 3$, the most direct thing is to write $(t-2)(t-3)$.
- Plug in $t=1$: $(1-2)(1-3) = (-1)(-2) = 2$.
- But we want $1$, not $2$, so divide by $2$ to normalize:

$$
L_1(t) = \frac{(t-2)(t-3)}{2}
$$

This $L_1(t)$ is the "switch for position 1": it's lit (value 1) at $t=1$ and off (value 0) at $t=2, 3$.

**Step 2**: Build $L_2(t)$ (only lit at $t=2$) and $L_3(t)$ (only lit at $t=3$) the same way:

$$
L_2(t) = \frac{(t-1)(t-3)}{(2-1)(2-3)} = -(t-1)(t-3)
$$

$$
L_3(t) = \frac{(t-1)(t-2)}{(3-1)(3-2)} = \frac{(t-1)(t-2)}{2}
$$

**Step 3**: Weight-sum the three switches by the y-values (the heights we want the curve to reach at each point):

$$
A_x(t) = 1 \cdot L_1(t) + 0 \cdot L_2(t) + 1 \cdot L_3(t) = \frac{(t-2)(t-3)}{2} + \frac{(t-1)(t-2)}{2}
$$

Simplify:

$$
= \frac{(t-2)\big[(t-3) + (t-1)\big]}{2} = \frac{(t-2)(2t-4)}{2} = (t-2)^2 = t^2 - 4t + 4
$$

So all those scary-looking fractions are each just a tiny "switch that lights up at one position and goes dark at the others." The whole Lagrange formula is "sum of switches."

</details>

## Step 3: every column becomes a polynomial

We've only been working with the $x$ column of matrix A.

In practice, we do the same thing for:
- Every column of matrix A
- Every column of matrix B
- Every column of matrix C

This gives three families of polynomials:

$$
A_i(t), \quad B_i(t), \quad C_i(t)
$$

where $i$ indexes the column for the $i$-th variable.

---

## Step 4: bring the witness in

Recall the witness vector:

$$
\vec{s} = [1, x, y, \text{sym}_1, \text{sym}_2]
$$

QAP uses the witness values to weight-combine the column polynomials into 3 aggregate polynomials:

$$
A(t) = \sum_i s_i \cdot A_i(t), \quad
B(t) = \sum_i s_i \cdot B_i(t), \quad
C(t) = \sum_i s_i \cdot C_i(t)
$$

If that looks abstract, here's it in one sentence:

> **Take each column's curve and weight-stack them by the corresponding witness value.**

This is just like the dot-product idea from the last section:
- Last section: "use coefficients to pick numbers out of the box"
- This section: "use witness values to combine many column curves"

So:
- $A(t)$ = the "aggregate left side" built from all A columns
- $B(t)$ = the "aggregate right side" built from all B columns
- $C(t)$ = the "aggregate result" built from all C columns

---

## Step 5: why can we pack many constraints into one equation?

The key QAP equation is:

$$
A(t) \cdot B(t) - C(t) = H(t) \cdot Z(t)
$$

Don't memorize it — first understand what it's saying.

### The left side represents "error"

$$
A(t) \cdot B(t) - C(t)
$$

If we were supposed to have:

$$
A(t) \cdot B(t) = C(t)
$$

then moving $C$ to the left gives 0.

So the left side is essentially:

> **the "total error function"**

If all constraints hold, this error is 0 at every constraint's position.

---

## Step 6: what is the vanishing polynomial?

QAP defines a very important polynomial:

$$
Z(t) = (t-1)(t-2)(t-3)
$$

Why this one?

Because our 3 constraints correspond to 3 evaluation points:

$$
t = 1,\; 2,\; 3
$$

so:

- At $t=1$: $Z(1)=0$
- At $t=2$: $Z(2)=0$
- At $t=3$: $Z(3)=0$

$Z(t)$ is a polynomial designed to vanish at exactly those "check points."

:::info Grade-school analogy
Think of $t=1,2,3$ as three gates.

$Z(t)$ is like a "special key":
- At gate 1, it becomes 0
- At gate 2, it also becomes 0
- At gate 3, still 0

If a polynomial is divisible by this "key," it must be zero at every one of those gates.
:::

---

## Step 7: what does divisibility tell us?

Revisit the equation:

$$
A(t) \cdot B(t) - C(t) = H(t) \cdot Z(t)
$$

What it really says is:

> **The error polynomial is divisible by $Z(t)$.**

And "divisible by $Z(t)$" is equivalent to:

> **The error polynomial is zero at every check point $t=1,2,3$.**

Which means:
- Constraint 1 holds
- Constraint 2 holds
- Constraint 3 holds

So this single divisibility relation already encodes "all constraints are satisfied."

---

## Saying it again, in the plainest terms

R1CS used to do:

```text
Check row 1
Check row 2
Check row 3
...
```

QAP changes that to:

```text
First turn every row into a polynomial
Then pack "zero everywhere it should be"
into "divisible by Z(t)"
```

So:

> **QAP isn't a different problem — it compresses "many small problems" into "one big problem."**

---

## Why is this more efficient?

```text
R1CS: check n constraints one by one
QAP:  check a single polynomial relation
```

Modern zk-SNARKs go further, using pairings and commitments to compress "check a polynomial relation" even more.

In the end, the verifier doesn't look at constraints one by one — only a very small amount of math.

### The key intuition

- **R1CS**: like a teacher grading problem by problem
- **QAP**: like a teacher summarizing all problems into one overall grading rule, and only checking that rule

---

## What actually happens at the check points?

If the witness is correct, at each check point:

### At $t=1$

$$
A(1) \cdot B(1) - C(1) = 0
$$

This means: **constraint 1 is satisfied**.

### At $t=2$

$$
A(2) \cdot B(2) - C(2) = 0
$$

This means: **constraint 2 is satisfied**.

### At $t=3$

$$
A(3) \cdot B(3) - C(3) = 0
$$

This means: **constraint 3 is satisfied**.

So if the expression is 0 at $t=1, 2, 3$, then:

$$
A(t) \cdot B(t) - C(t)
$$

must contain the factor:

$$
(t-1)(t-2)(t-3)
$$

i.e.:

$$
Z(t)
$$

That's the heart of QAP.

---

## Three sentences to remember QAP

1. **Turn every column of R1CS numbers into a polynomial curve.**
2. **Combine all the constraint errors into one total error polynomial.**
3. **If the total error is zero at every check point, it must be divisible by $Z(t)$.**

---

## Why does the verifier only need to check very little?

The important result here is the **Schwartz-Zippel Lemma**.

Roughly:

> If two different polynomials are evaluated at a random point, they are very unlikely to happen to agree.

So the verifier doesn't need to expand and inspect the whole polynomial.
Checking the relation at a single random point is enough — a bogus proof almost cannot sneak past.

One way to think about it:

> **A fake polynomial has a very hard time disguising itself as the real one under a random spot check.**

---

## The grand analogy

- **Arithmetic circuit**: like a step-by-step problem diagram
- **R1CS**: like turning each step into a checklist item
- **QAP**: like compressing all checklist items into one overall rule
- **zk-SNARK**: like using that one rule to quickly prove "I got everything right" without revealing any intermediate details
