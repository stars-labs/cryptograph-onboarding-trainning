## Why do we need QAP?

R1CS checks constraints one by one—you have to verify as many times as there are multiplication gates. QAP (Quadratic Arithmetic Program) "compresses" all constraints into **a single polynomial equation**, allowing verification in one step.

## Lagrange Interpolation

Core idea: Convert each **column** of the A, B, and C matrices into a polynomial.

Taking the 2nd column of matrix A (corresponding to variable $x$) as an example:

$$
A_{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

The values at the three points $\{1, 2, 3\}$ (corresponding to the three constraints) are $1, 0, 1$ respectively.

Use Lagrange interpolation to find the polynomial $A_x(t)$ that passes through these points:

$$
A_x(t) = 1 \cdot \frac{(t-2)(t-3)}{(1-2)(1-3)} + 0 \cdot \frac{(t-1)(t-3)}{(2-1)(2-3)} + 1 \cdot \frac{(t-1)(t-2)}{(3-1)(3-2)}
$$

$$
A_x(t) = \frac{(t-2)(t-3)}{2} + \frac{(t-1)(t-2)}{2} = t^2 - 3t + 3
$$

Perform the same operation for every column of A, B, and C to get a set of polynomials.

## QAP Core Equation

Combine all column polynomials with the witness vector:

$$
A(t) = \sum_i s_i \cdot A_i(t), \quad B(t) = \sum_i s_i \cdot B_i(t), \quad C(t) = \sum_i s_i \cdot C_i(t)
$$

If the witness satisfies all R1CS constraints, then:

$$
A(t) \cdot B(t) - C(t) = H(t) \cdot Z(t)
$$

Where $Z(t) = (t-1)(t-2)(t-3)$ is the "vanishing polynomial", which is zero at each constraint point. $H(t)$ is the quotient polynomial.

:::info Intuitive Understanding
- At $t = 1$: $A(1) \cdot B(1) - C(1) = 0$, which is exactly satisfying constraint 1
- At $t = 2$: $A(2) \cdot B(2) - C(2) = 0$, which is exactly satisfying constraint 2
- At $t = 3$: $A(3) \cdot B(3) - C(3) = 0$, which is exactly satisfying constraint 3

So $A(t) \cdot B(t) - C(t)$ is zero at $t = 1, 2, 3$, meaning it is divisible by $(t-1)(t-2)(t-3)$!
:::

## Why is the polynomial form more efficient?

```
R1CS: Check n constraints one by one    → O(n) verifications
QAP: Check one polynomial divisibility relation → O(1) verification (with pairing operations)
```

The **Schwartz-Zippel Lemma** tells us: if two polynomials have the same value at a random point, they are extremely likely to be the same polynomial. Therefore, the verifier only needs to check the equation at a single (encrypted) random point!
